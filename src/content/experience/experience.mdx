## The Work

I've spent the better part of two decades building products at the intersection of complex systems and the people who operate them. That's meant AR overlays on a rocket assembly floor, VR environments where engineers could walk through a vehicle before it existed in metal, and training simulators that put astronauts face-to-face with hardware they'd never physically touch until they were in orbit.

The throughline: I'm drawn to problems where the gap between what people *know* and what they need to *do* can be closed with the right tool.

At Blue Origin, I worked where the hardware gets built — on the floor, with the engineers and technicians who assemble space vehicles. My job was to figure out where XR could genuinely help and then get it there.

That meant shaping the roadmap for AR/VR initiatives, managing a Jira backlog across multiple stakeholder teams, and running VR sessions where engineers could evaluate installation approaches in 3D before committing to them in metal. We caught design and workflow problems earlier, and in 2–3 production work cells, technicians used AR overlays during final assembly — contributing to roughly 10–15% faster assembly times.

The part I valued most: learning to say no to low-impact use cases. Not every problem needs XR. Getting good at that judgment made the wins count more.

</ExperienceEntry>

Beyond keeping the lights on, I facilitated roadmap sessions with instructors, engineers, and operations staff to plan the next generation of training tools. It sharpened my understanding of how training infrastructure has to flex around the rhythm of real missions.

  company="Teledyne Brown Engineering / NASA"
  period="2007 – 2022"
  location="Huntsville, AL"

Fifteen years building training tools for the International Space Station program. I started making physical hardware mockups and ended up building virtual environments — which is a pretty good summary of how the field moved during that time.

The flagship work was the Glass Rack Trainer system: digital representations of ISS hardware that trainees couldn't physically access. They gave flight controllers and astronauts meaningful familiarity with equipment before live operations, and we deployed them to 3–4 NASA sites for astronaut training, flight controller training, and public outreach.

I also built AR-based training setups that let instructors run sessions with students in remote locations — a capability that turned out to matter a lot more when the world went remote in 2020. Toward the end of this chapter, I built Power BI dashboards to track facility usage and help leadership make better decisions about resource allocation.

The through-line across all of it: end-to-end ownership. Requirements, design, build, deployment, instructor onboarding — and then iterating based on what actually happened in the room.

</ExperienceEntry>

On the technical side, I built a functional MVP using Next.js, Cesium.js, and Supabase to validate feasibility and give stakeholders something real to react to before engineering investment.

Working at this scale — fast, scrappy, high-stakes — has been a good complement to the methodical pace of aerospace programs.
